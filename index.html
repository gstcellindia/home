GST Notice Examiner – Usage Guide

Basic Review Prompt

When a GST notice, investigation report, or GST order is uploaded, enter:

“Review this GST Show Cause Notice draft and list all errors.”

The tool will identify mistakes and generate a structured report.

Advanced Review Prompt

For deeper analysis and a more comprehensive report, enter:

“Act as a senior GST Investigation Expert and legal drafting analyst. Review GST notices, investigation reports, statements, submissions, and adjudication orders for:

  • Linguistic issues – grammar, clarity, sentence structure
  • Legal issues – Act/Rule violations, incorrect section citations, limitation, natural justice concerns
  • Factual inconsistencies – GSTIN, period, amounts, returns, annexures
  • Computational errors – tax, interest, penalty, totals
  • Procedural lapses – improper SCN, missing documents, invalid authority"

The tool will then produce a structured report containing findings, corrections, and recommendations.

Bank Statement Reconciliation – Usage Guide

Goal

To analyze a taxpayer’s bank statement and surface anomalies, discrepancies, and GST-fraud risk indicators, written as investigation-ready leads (not accusations).

Review the provided bank statement data and produce a structured “Anomaly & GST Risk Report”

Context

  • - Bank statement (CSV/Excel/text table): Date, Value Date, Narration, Cheque/UTR, Debit, Credit, Balance, Channel (NEFT/RTGS/IMPS/UPI/Cash), Counterparty (if present).
  • - Taxpayer profile (if available): GSTIN, legal name, business nature, declared turnover, filing status, principal place of business, key vendors/customers.
  • - Optional: GSTR-1, GSTR-3B summaries, e-way bill summaries, vendor/customer master list, prior intelligence inputs.

Step-by-step instructions:

(1) Clean and standardize: normalize dates, amounts, deduplicate, flag missing fields, classify each transaction (sales receipt, purchase payment, cash deposit/withdrawal, self- transfer, loan, refund, tax payment, unknown).

(2) Build baselines: monthly totals, average ticket size, top counterparties, cash ratio, inflow/outflow symmetry, pass-through behavior, round-number frequency, spikes/outliers.

(3) Detect red flags linked to GST fraud risks, such as:

  • - Turnover mismatch signals (bank credits vs declared turnover, sharp seasonality shifts).
  • - Circular/round-tripping patterns (in-and-out same/next day, same amounts, clustered counter parties).
  • - Layering/splitting (many small transfers below typical thresholds, repetitive UPI/IMPS).
  • - High cash activity inconsistent with business profile, sudden cash deposits before tax dates.
  • - Payments to high-risk/unknown entities, frequent “loan/advance” narratives, refunds/chargebacks patterns.

(4) Correlate: map each anomaly to plausible GST risk themes (fake invoicing/ITC, suppression of sales, accommodation entries) and state what additional evidence would confirm/refute it.


(5) Produce the report in this exact format:

  • (A) Executive summary (5–8 bullets).
  • (B) Anomaly Register table with columns: ID, Date range, Pattern, Transactions/Evidence (txn references), Why it’s anomalous, GST risk linkage, Severity (1–5), Data gaps, Suggested follow-up.
  • (C) Counter party risk list (top 15) with brief notes.
  • (D) Questions to issue to the taxpayer (10–15 precise questions).
  • (E) Next actions checklist (bank confirmation, summons targets, return reconciliation steps).
  • (F) Caveats: do not allege guilt; treat as leads; do not guess missing facts—mark “Unknown”.
